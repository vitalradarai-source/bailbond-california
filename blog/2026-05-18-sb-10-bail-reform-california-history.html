California-Specific Facts You Need

SB-10, signed into law by Governor Brown in August 2018, was scheduled to take effect October 2019. It would have ended cash bail in California, replacing it with a risk-assessment algorithm administered by county pretrial agencies. The bail bond industry challenged the law via referendum, qualifying Proposition 25 for the November 2020 ballot — a vote to either uphold (Yes) or reject (No) SB-10.

Proposition 25 failed 56% to 44%. The result: California's cash-bail system remained intact. Bail schedules per county still govern bail amounts. Licensed bondsmen still post surety bonds at the 10% statutory premium under Cal Insurance Code §1800.4. Risk-assessment tools exist in some counties (LA's Pretrial Services Division, SF's Public Safety Assessment) but their recommendations are advisory — judges retain discretion at arraignment.

Where SB-10's defeat left things: California continues to operate under PC §1268-1320 (the bail statutes), with case law from In re Humphrey (2021) Cal. 5th 135 adding constitutional limits — judges must consider ability-to-pay before setting cash bail. Humphrey didn't eliminate bail; it required judges to think about whether a defendant can actually pay before imposing a number. The practical effect: bail-bond demand for bonds in the $10,000-$100,000 range grew, since judges can no longer simply default to schedule amounts without inquiry.

For families navigating California bail in 2026, the operative rule remains: posting a 10% bail bond is faster and more economical than waiting for an OR (own-recognizance) release evaluation. Pretrial services in most counties take 24-72 hours to complete their evaluation; a bond posted at booking can release a defendant in 4-12 hours.

What SB-10 / Prop 25 Actually Says

Senate Bill 10 (2018) and Proposition 25 (2020) covers: Attempted abolition of cash bail in favor of risk-assessment release; rejected by voters Nov 2020. Understanding the statute matters because the bail schedule, no-bail-hold rules, and protective-order rules all flow from how the prosecutor charges the case.

Three points families typically miss in SB-10 / Prop 25 cases: the charging decision is not final at booking — the arresting agency's charge can change at the DA's filing review (within 48 hours under PC §825). Bail schedule amounts are not ceilings — a judge at arraignment can raise bail above the schedule based on flight risk or danger, or lower it on defense motion under In re Humphrey (2021). Protective orders can attach automatically — under PC §136.2, a Criminal Protective Order can issue at arraignment with a 100-yard stay-away clause that may bar the defendant from returning home.

Bail Cost and the 10% Premium

The bail amount and the cost to post a bond are two different numbers. The bail amount is the dollar figure the court requires to guarantee appearance — set per the statewide California bail schedule and subject to judicial discretion at arraignment.

The cost to a family is the 10% statutory premium under California Insurance Code §1800.4. This premium is a fee for the surety service, not money lent to the customer — it is non-refundable regardless of case outcome (acquittal, dismissal, conviction, plea). The 10% rate is set by California Department of Insurance regulation; no licensed bondsman can charge a different rate. A bondsman quoting 5% or 8% is either operating outside the law or planning to add fees later.

Mistakes to Avoid in the First 24 Hours

  1. Don't wait for arraignment to act. Bail can be posted as soon as booking is complete — typically within 2-6 hours of arrest.
  2. Don't pay cash bail without checking with a bondsman first. Cash bail ties up the full bail amount until case resolution.
  3. Don't sign indemnitor paperwork you haven't read. The indemnitor agreement is a binding contract.
  4. Don't coach the defendant on what to say at booking. Phone calls from jail are recorded.
  5. Don't violate a protective order, even verbally. If a PC §136.2 CPO issues at arraignment, contact through any means can result in a new charge under PC §166.

How long does it take to post bail at the appropriate county jail?

Is SB-10 / Prop 25 a bondable offense?

Most SB-10 / Prop 25 cases are bondable per the county schedule. Exceptions: no-bail holds from separate active cases, parole holds, immigration detainers, or specific judicial orders.

Can the protective order be modified?

Yes. The protected party can request modification at arraignment or any subsequent hearing.

What happens if the case is dismissed?

The bail bond is exonerated and the obligation ends. The 10% premium is non-refundable per California Insurance Code §1800.4.

Do I have to use a bondsman in statewide California?

No. Families can post cash bail directly. The tradeoffs: cash bail ties up the full amount until case resolution; bond costs 10% but is non-refundable.
