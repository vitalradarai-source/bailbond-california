California Domestic Violence Bail Bonds: A Complete Statewide Guide

When someone is arrested for domestic violence in California, their family has one urgent question: how fast can they come home? The answer depends on understanding how California's bail system works for DV charges — which county made the arrest, which jail is holding them, and what the bail schedule says. This guide covers the full process from arrest through release, with specific detail on the two largest markets: Los Angeles County and Orange County.

California's Mandatory Arrest Policy for Domestic Violence

California law requires officers to make an arrest whenever they have probable cause to believe a DV offense has occurred — even without a formal victim complaint. If responding officers see evidence of a physical altercation, visible injuries, or a distressed victim, they are required to take someone into custody. This mandatory arrest policy means DV arrests happen fast and are not reversed at the scene.

The two charges that follow most California DV arrests are:

  • PC § 273.5 — Corporal injury to a spouse, cohabitant, or co-parent. A "wobbler" — filed as felony or misdemeanor depending on injury severity and criminal history. Most first offenses with visible injury are charged as felonies.
  • PC § 243(e)(1) — Domestic battery. Typically a misdemeanor, charged when no visible injury is present but a battery occurred.

No Mandatory Hold in California

The most important fact for any family to know: California does not have a mandatory holding period for domestic violence arrests. Bail can be posted as soon as booking is complete and the charge is confirmed in the system. The person does not have to wait for a bail hearing — schedule bail is set automatically from the county bail schedule the moment they are booked.

Common myth: Many families are told there is a 72-hour hold for DV in California. This is false. There is no mandatory hold under California law. Bail is available from the bail schedule immediately after booking completes.

LA County: Where the Defendant Is Held

Los Angeles County operates the largest jail system in the United States. Where someone is held after a DV arrest depends on the arresting agency and booking volume at the time:

  • Twin Towers Correctional Facility (450 Bauchet St, Los Angeles) — primary intake for LAPD and central LASD arrests. One of the largest detention facilities in the world.
  • Men's Central Jail (441 Bauchet St, Los Angeles) — adjacent to Twin Towers; handles overflow and longer-term pre-trial housing.
  • Regional LASD Stations — contract cities and unincorporated areas book locally first (e.g., Industry Station for East SGV, Lakewood Station for Southeast LA County). The defendant may remain at the station or transfer to central facilities.

Arraignment for most LA County DV arrests is at the Clara Shortridge Foltz Criminal Justice Center (210 W Temple St, Los Angeles) or the regional district courthouse based on arrest location — Pomona Courthouse for East SGV, Compton Courthouse for Southeast LA, Van Nuys Courthouse for the Valley.

Orange County: Where the Defendant Is Held

Orange County routes DV bookings to one of two main facilities depending on location:

  • Theo Lacy Facility (501 The City Drive South, Orange) — North and Central OC arrests.
  • James A. Musick Facility (13502 Musick Ave, Irvine) — Central and South OC arrests.

Arraignment takes place at the Central Justice Center (700 Civic Center Drive West, Santa Ana) for most OC cases, or the Harbor Justice Center (4601 Jamboree Rd, Newport Beach) for South Orange County arrests.

How to Post a Bail Bond for a California DV Arrest

The bail bond process is standardized across California. A licensed bondsman charges a state-regulated premium of 10% of the bail amount — the same rate in every county, set by the California Department of Insurance. This is non-negotiable.

To start, you need:

  • The arrested person's full legal name and date of birth
  • The arresting agency and county (LAPD, LASD, local PD)
  • The booking number if available — this speeds up the search significantly
  • A co-signer (indemnitor) with a valid ID willing to take financial responsibility

Criminal Protective Orders: What Happens After Release

In virtually every California DV arrest, an Emergency Protective Order (EPO) is issued at the scene by the arresting officer. The EPO takes effect immediately and restricts the defendant from contacting or returning to the home of the alleged victim. EPOs typically last 5 to 7 days.

At arraignment, the court typically replaces the EPO with a Criminal Protective Order (CPO) lasting through the duration of the case — sometimes years. The defendant must comply with the CPO as a condition of bail. Violating it is a separate crime under PC § 273.6 and triggers immediate re-arrest and potential bail forfeiture.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is there a 72-hour hold for domestic violence in California?

No. This is a widespread myth. California law does not require any holding period before bail can be posted for a DV arrest. Once booking is complete and the charge is in the system, a bondsman can post bail immediately.

Can a judge deny bail for a domestic violence charge in California?

Yes, in limited circumstances. A judge can increase bail above the schedule amount or deny it entirely if the defendant is found to pose a danger to the alleged victim or the community. This typically applies to cases involving weapons, serious injury, or a pattern of prior DV offenses. For most first-offense DV charges, schedule bail applies without a hearing.

Does the victim dropping charges affect the bail bond?

No — not directly. The District Attorney, not the victim, controls prosecution in California. Charges may continue even if the victim requests dismissal. The bail bond remains active until the court formally closes the case. Once the case concludes, the bail bond is exonerated and the obligation ends.

How long does release take after a bail bond is posted?

At Twin Towers in LA, expect 4 to 8 hours from bond posting to release. Smaller county jails and regional sheriff's stations often process releases faster — sometimes 1 to 3 hours. Orange County facilities vary by current population levels.

What is the bail bond cost for a $50,000 DV bail in California?

The premium is 10%, so $5,000. This rate is fixed by California law. Angels Bail Bonds offers payment arrangements for qualified co-signers — the full premium does not always have to be paid at once.
